# Why We Build

Most communities do not have an information problem. They have a fragmentation problem.

Useful context already exists in open tabs, meeting notes, shared links, photos, and half-finished
documents. The problem is that it rarely becomes shared memory at the moment a group needs it.

## The Problem We Are Aiming At

When context stays scattered:

- research gets rediscovered instead of reused
- funding leads lose momentum before they become concrete next steps
- working groups repeat each other's work
- collective memory weakens between calls, rituals, and planning cycles
- evidence for action exists, but not in a form the group can coordinate around

Coop is meant to be the membrane between private fragments and shared understanding.

## Why Local-First Matters

The product choice is not just technical taste. Communities working on governance, funding, impact,
or sensitive field knowledge need clear ownership over what is local and what is shared.

That is why Coop starts from a simple rule: local by default, explicit when shared.

This posture aligns with the broader values the repo points to in the Ethereum Foundation's March
13, 2026 mandate:

- self-sovereignty over identities, actions, and agents
- privacy as a default property, not an afterthought
- open and forkable infrastructure instead of a locked platform
- coordination that does not depend on a central actor owning the whole graph

## Why The Product Story Is Social, Not Just Technical

Coop is for groups that already know what it feels like to have valuable context but no dependable
place for it to mature:

- regenerative communities and bioregional networks
- research and grant coordination teams
- contributors trying to turn scattered evidence into fundable proposals
- smaller trusted groups that want durable memory without defaulting to surveillance-heavy tools

The goal is not to build a generic note bucket. It is to help communities move from "we all saw
something important" to "we can act on this together."

## The Standard We Hold Ourselves To

If Coop is working, a group should feel three things:

- it can capture useful signal without losing privacy boundaries
- it can review and publish shared knowledge without a complicated setup ceremony
- it can keep that knowledge portable enough to survive changes in tools, maintainers, or hosting

That is the standard this project holds itself to.
