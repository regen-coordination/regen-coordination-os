# Core Mental Models — Expert-Feynman

## 1. First Principles Decomposition

Strip away inherited assumptions and rebuild from fundamental truths.

**Process:**
1. State the accepted truth
2. Ask "Is this actually true?"
3. Ask "What do we KNOW vs. what do we ASSUME?"
4. Rebuild from verified fundamentals

**Example (DAO governance):**
- Accepted: "DAOs need token voting"
- First principles: What coordination problem are we solving?
- If the problem is "allocate a budget monthly," maybe token voting is overkill
- If the problem is "resolve disputes between anonymous parties," maybe it fits

---

## 2. The Feynman Technique

Explain a concept in simple terms to identify gaps.

**Steps:**
1. Choose a concept
2. Explain it as if teaching a beginner (no jargon)
3. Identify where you get stuck or use vague language
4. Return to source material for those gaps
5. Simplify further

**Use in org-os:**
- Explain the federation protocol to a new contributor
- Explain a governance proposal to someone outside the org
- Explain a technical architecture to a non-technical operator

---

## 3. Multiple Representations

Understand the same thing in multiple ways.

**Representations:**
- Algebraic (equations, formal logic)
- Geometric (diagrams, spatial relationships)
- Physical (analogies, embodied understanding)
- Intuitive (gut feeling, pattern recognition)

**Rule:** If you only know one representation, you don't really understand.

**Use in org-os:**
- Show federation as a graph (geometric), as protocols (algebraic), and as relationships (intuitive)
- Explain token economics through equations, flow diagrams, AND a story about a village market

---

## 4. Active Debugging

Scientific method applied to problem-solving.

**Process:**
1. Form a specific, falsifiable hypothesis
2. Design the cheapest experiment to test it
3. Run the experiment
4. If wrong, form a new hypothesis (don't patch the old one)
5. If right, you've found a piece of truth

**Difference from guessing:**
- Guessing: "Maybe it's X, let me try changing X"
- Active debugging: "I believe it's X because Y. If I'm right, changing Z should produce W. Let me test."

---

## 5. The Joy of Finding Things Out

Curiosity as a driver of deep understanding.

**Attitudes:**
- "I don't know, and that's exciting"
- "Let's find out together"
- "The answer is probably simpler than we think"
- "Mystery is better than false certainty"

**Warning signs of losing this:**
- Defending a position rather than seeking truth
- Feeling threatened by questions
- Preferring complex answers because they sound more sophisticated

---

## 6. What Do You Care What Other People Think?

Independence of judgment.

**Core idea:** The truth doesn't depend on who believes it or how many people agree.

**Applications:**
- Evaluating governance proposals on merit, not popularity
- Choosing technology based on fit, not hype
- Making decisions based on evidence, not social pressure

**Balance:** This is not contrarianism for its own sake. It's independence — willingness to agree when the evidence supports it AND disagree when it doesn't.
